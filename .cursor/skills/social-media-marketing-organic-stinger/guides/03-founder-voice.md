# Guide 03: Founder Voice

The voice-finding exercise. The goal is to surface how the founder already talks, not to impose a brand voice template.

---

## The core insight

"Document your process, not just your highlights." — For The Love of Socials

Founders underestimate how much authentic material they are producing every day in private contexts: Slack messages to their team, replies to customer emails, explanations in onboarding calls, DMs to peers. The authentic voice is already there. The exercise is to find it and translate it into post format without sanitizing the personality out.

AI can write about your product. It cannot write about the specific customer conversation you had on a Tuesday, the investor call that went sideways, or the feature you quietly deleted because nobody used it.

---

## The voice-mining exercise

Run this with every founder before writing a single piece of content.

### Step 1: Gather raw material

Ask the founder to find examples of themselves writing or speaking naturally — not marketing copy:

1. **Slack or team chat messages** from the last 30 days. Look for explanations, rants, excited messages about something that worked. Filter for messages where the founder was explaining something complex to a non-technical person.
2. **Customer support replies** or onboarding emails they wrote personally (not templated).
3. **DMs or text messages** where they explained their product or problem space to a friend.
4. **Meeting recordings or transcripts** (internal product discussions, customer calls) where they spoke off-script.

If the founder doesn't have these, ask them to describe their product in one paragraph as if to a curious 12-year-old. Record it verbatim, including false starts.

### Step 2: Identify register markers

Read through the raw material and look for:

- **Vocabulary patterns:** What unusual words do they use? What technical terms do they avoid?
- **Sentence length:** Do they write in bursts (short sentences) or do they explain in long subordinate clauses?
- **Hedging vs directness:** Do they say "it kind of does X" or "it does X"?
- **Humor style:** Self-deprecating? Dry? Enthusiastic? Absent entirely?
- **What they complain about:** The things that frustrate the founder genuinely are some of their best content.

### Step 3: Extract three sentences that sound unmistakably like them

From the raw material, find three sentences that would never survive a brand voice filter but are immediately recognizable as this founder. These become the voice anchors.

Example voice anchor (from a founder's Slack): "Honestly the feature we built in March is embarrassing and I'm glad nobody uses it."

That sentence is a great LinkedIn post. It already exists. It just needs a context wrap.

### Step 4: Translate, don't sanitize

Take the three voice anchors and turn each one into a post scaffold. The rules:

1. Do NOT rewrite the vocabulary into "cleaner" language. Keep the founder's words even if they're casual.
2. DO add context (who is reading this, why does it matter to them).
3. DO remove anything legally risky (customer names, confidential numbers, competitor slurs).
4. DO add one piece of specificity that only this founder could write.

### Step 5: Platform register calibration

The same underlying voice needs different packaging per platform:

| Platform | Register | Post format |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn | Slightly more professional, but personal | 150-400 words, paragraph-heavy, strong opening line |
| X | Punchy, direct, no filler | 280 chars or 5-8 tweet thread, numbers up front |
| Bluesky | Direct, dev-community tone, can be blunt | 1-3 paragraphs, links in text are fine |
| Threads | Conversational, short, reaction-seeking | 1-3 sentences, no hashtag spam |

---

## Common voice pathologies and fixes

**Pathology 1: "Professional mode"**
Founder sounds like a LinkedIn bot. Everything is "excited to share" and "thrilled to announce." Fix: ask them to write the same post as a Slack message to a close friend. Use that draft.

**Pathology 2: Too technical**
Post reads as an engineering spec. No non-technical reader can follow. Fix: add "what this means for the person reading it" at the end. Drop half the technical terms.

**Pathology 3: Too hedged**
"Kind of," "sort of," "I think maybe," "it's possible that." Fix: directness is a voice asset, not arrogance. Remove every hedge that doesn't serve a genuine epistemic purpose.

**Pathology 4: No personal data anchors**
Post reads as generic insight with no specificity. Could have been written about any product. Fix: add one sentence with a date, a specific number, a person's role, or a concrete situation. "In our first week after launch" is better than "early on." "Three enterprise customers told us" is better than "customers told us."
