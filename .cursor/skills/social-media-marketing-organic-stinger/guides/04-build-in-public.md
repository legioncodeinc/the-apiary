# Guide 04: Build-in-Public Playbook

The BIP methodology for solo founders on X, Bluesky, and LinkedIn.

---

## The 70/30 rule

The most common BIP mistake is posting only about the product. "We shipped X," "feature Y is live," "we hit milestone Z." This quickly becomes noise that audiences tune out.

**The 70/30 rule (Founder Distro 2026):**
- 70% of posts: useful insights and observations for anyone — whether they buy or not
- 30% of posts: direct product and company updates

The 70% is what grows the audience. The 30% is what converts them.

For X specifically, a more granular breakdown applies: **70-20-10 rule** (Monolit 2026):
- 70% value content: insights, frameworks, lessons from the actual journey
- 20% engagement content: replies, quote tweets, participating in conversations
- 10% promotional content: product updates, feature launches, direct CTAs

## What to share

### Revenue and business numbers
Specific numbers make abstract progress concrete. The most engaging build-in-public posts contain actual figures — not approximate ranges.

Good: "MRR hit $3,847 yesterday. 23 paying customers. Churn is 0 so far but I know that won't last."
Bad: "We're growing steadily and things are going well!"

What to share: MRR, ARR, DAU, MAU, churn rate, conversion rates, customer interview counts, NPS scores, support ticket volume, deployment frequency.

The number does not need to be impressive. A founder going from $0 to $100 MRR is more interesting to the audience than a press release about $10M ARR — it is a story in progress.

### Decisions and their rationale
Product decisions are some of the most useful content for the audience. "We decided to ship X instead of Y because..." is simultaneously a product update, a strategic insight, and a community discussion starter.

Examples:
- "We cut the feature that took 3 months to build. Here's why."
- "We changed our pricing from per-seat to flat-rate. What we learned."
- "We almost pivoted. Here's the conversation that stopped us."

### Failures and learning moments
**Failure posts outperform success posts 3-5x on X** (Founder Distro 2026). This is not invitation to manufacture misery or pretend every failure is a learning. Post real failures as learning moments.

What counts as a real failure: launches that flopped, features nobody uses, pricing strategies that backfired, hiring mistakes, wrong customer segment assumptions.

The post structure: what happened → what I expected → what actually happened → what I changed.

### Process documentation
The founder doing something interesting is content. The commute call where they validated a pricing assumption. The three hours they spent debugging a Stripe webhook. The spreadsheet they built to prioritize the roadmap.

"Document your process, not just your highlights" is the operational definition of build-in-public.

## What NOT to share

### Legal exposure
Customer names without permission, ongoing negotiations, competitor agreements, anything under NDA. "A Fortune 500 company" is fine. "Microsoft's internal process for evaluating vendors" is not.

### Customer PII
No names, email addresses, company names (without permission), or identifiable support conversations.

### Uncontextualized failure
A failure post without the "what I learned / changed" context is not build-in-public — it is a cry for help. Audiences support contextualized failure and disengage from uncontextualized despair.

### Live stress without perspective
Venting in real-time about a crisis is understandable but rarely lands well. Write the post a day after the crisis when you have perspective.

## Platform-specific BIP formats

### X (Twitter)
| Format | Best for |
|---|---|
| Thread (5-8 tweets) | "Here's what I learned from 100 customer interviews" / breakdown posts |
| Single-tweet observation | One specific, surprising observation in 280 chars. No expansion needed. |
| Revenue/metrics update | Monthly or quarterly. Numbers first, then context. 5-8 tweets. |

Post timing (2026 data): Weekdays 8-10 AM EST. Tuesday/Wednesday highest engagement for founder and B2B content.

**X algorithm note:** Engage with your audience immediately after posting. Reply to every comment. Ask questions to seed conversation. Early velocity (first 30 minutes) is the primary distribution signal.

### LinkedIn
- Long-form process breakdown: 300-400 words, one strong opening line
- Failure+learning post: 200-300 words, lead with the failure
- Monthly numbers update: bullet points with context, no jargon

**LinkedIn note:** External links in LinkedIn posts suppress reach. Write the post content natively; add the link in the first comment.

### Bluesky
- Direct, shorter format: 1-3 paragraphs
- Dev community appreciates technical specificity
- Links are fine in the post body (no suppression)
- Thread structure similar to X but with looser formatting

---

## The first 90 days of BIP

**Week 1:** Set a baseline. Share where you are right now — what you are building, where you are in the journey, the one problem you are most focused on solving this month. Include numbers however small.

**Month 2-3:** Find your voice. Figure out which format the audience responds to most. Double down on that while keeping variety alive.

**Month 3+:** The cadence and voice become habitual. The audience starts engaging before you finish the post.
