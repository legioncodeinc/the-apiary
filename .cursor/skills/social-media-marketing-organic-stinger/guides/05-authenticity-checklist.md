# Guide 05: Authenticity Checklist

12-point checklist a post must pass before publishing. Apply before every post, not just when something feels off.

---

## The checklist

### 1. Could an AI have written this?

Read the post. Could ChatGPT have written exactly this post about any product? If yes: add one sentence that only this founder, on this day, with this specific customer situation, could have written.

Fail signal: no dates, no names (even anonymized roles like "a VP of Engineering told me"), no specific numbers, no personal moment.

### 2. Does it contain at least one specific detail no AI could fabricate?

This is the single highest-signal authenticity check. A specific detail could be: a revenue number, a date, a customer's role and what they said, a concrete feature behavior, a specific mistake the founder made.

"Three weeks ago" is better than "recently."
"$847 MRR" is better than "early revenue."
"A VP of Engineering at a fintech startup told me" is better than "a customer told me."

**Research grounding:** `research/external/2026-05-20-ai-content-detection-linkedin.md` (LinkedIn 360Brew scans for personal data density)

### 3. Does it sound like how the founder actually talks?

Run the voice anchor test from `guides/03-founder-voice.md`. Compare the post to the founder's Slack messages or customer email replies. If the post sounds dramatically more "professional" or "branded" — it has lost the authentic register.

### 4. Does it have natural contractions?

AI-generated text skews toward formal: "do not," "it is," "we are." Human writing skews toward contractions: "don't," "it's," "we're." Under 4 contractions in a 200-word post is a yellow flag on LinkedIn (360Brew signal).

### 5. Does it start with something other than "Excited to announce" or "I'm thrilled to share"?

These phrases are the most common AI-generated opening signals. Flag every post that starts with them. Stronger alternatives: start with the number, start with the failure, start with the counterintuitive observation, start with the question.

### 6. Does it avoid more than two em-dashes?

LinkedIn's 360Brew model flags posts with more than two em-dashes and zero ellipses as likely AI-generated. This is a stylistic signal. Replace em-dashes with commas, periods, or colons.

### 7. Is the call-to-action (if any) specific and single?

Posts with multiple CTAs convert worse than posts with one clear ask. More importantly, posts with vague "engagement bait" CTAs (like and share if you agree!) signal inauthenticity. If there's a CTA, it should be a specific action — "reply with your use case," "I'm answering questions for the next hour," "link in first comment."

### 8. Would the founder be embarrassed if a valued customer read this?

Not "would a random person agree with this" — the question is whether the post represents the founder accurately to the people who matter most. Inflammatory, vague, or content-farmy posts fail this test.

### 9. Does it generate substantive comments, not just likes?

Before publishing, predict what kind of engagement this post will attract. A post that attracts only likes is algorithmically weaker than one that attracts replies. If the post has no obvious question, debate point, or surprising claim — it will likely get passive approval, not conversation.

**Research grounding:** `research/external/2026-05-20-anti-ai-backlash-algorithms.md` ("50 substantive comments outranks 500 passive likes")

### 10. Is it platform-native?

Does the post fit the platform's format and audience expectations? LinkedIn post with 15 hashtags — no. X post with 400 words — no. Bluesky post with corporate tone — no. See `guides/02-platform-selection.md` for platform register reference.

### 11. Does it respect the 70/30 rule?

Is this post in the "70% valuable-to-anyone" category or the "30% product update" category? Track the ratio over the past month. If more than 30% of posts have been product announcements, the queue is unbalanced.

### 12. Is this a post the founder can defend in a DM?

Social media posts sometimes generate DMs. Would the founder be able to back up every claim, explain every number, and stand behind every word if someone asked "what did you mean by this?" If not, revise until they can.

---

## Quick reference (card-format)

```
Before posting, check:
[ ] 1. Not AI-writeable without the specific details
[ ] 2. Contains at least one fabrication-proof specific
[ ] 3. Sounds like the founder's actual voice
[ ] 4. Natural contractions present (4+)
[ ] 5. Does not start with "Excited to" or "Thrilled to"
[ ] 6. Fewer than 3 em-dashes
[ ] 7. One specific CTA (if any)
[ ] 8. Customer-embarrassment test passed
[ ] 9. Will generate comments, not just likes
[ ] 10. Platform-native format and register
[ ] 11. 70/30 ratio maintained
[ ] 12. Can defend every claim in a DM
```

The portable version of this checklist lives at `templates/authenticity-checklist.md`.
