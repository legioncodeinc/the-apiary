# Benefits Brokerage — Startup Benefits Setup

Use this guide when the user's company is setting up employee benefits for the first time or re-evaluating their benefits package. Output: benefits strategy recommendation with platform path.

---

## ACA triggers (know these before recommending)

| Threshold | Obligation |
|---|---|
| 1-49 full-time equivalent (FTE) employees | ACA does NOT require you to offer health coverage (but you should for talent) |
| 50+ FTE employees | ACA mandates minimum essential health coverage or face penalties (~$2,970/FTE/year over threshold) |
| 100+ FTE | Subject to ACA large employer reporting requirements (Forms 1094-C, 1095-C) |

Most early-stage startups are under 50 FTE and choose to offer benefits voluntarily for competitive hiring.

## Benefits setup options by stage

### Stage 1: Pre-product-market-fit (1-5 employees)

**Best option:** No group health plan. Use ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA) instead.

- **ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA):** You set a monthly allowance (e.g., $500-600/employee/month). Employees buy their own individual health plans on the exchange or marketplace. You reimburse them up to the allowance, tax-free.
- **Why ICHRA at this stage:** No group plan administration overhead. Employees choose their own plan. Fully flexible for remote-first teams across different states (group plan networks are geography-dependent).
- **Platforms for ICHRA:** PeopleKeep, Take Command Health, Thatch (newer entrant with better UX)
- **IRS limit for 2026:** Individual ICHRA has no statutory cap; QSEHRA (for companies without group plans) is capped at $6,350/year individual / $12,800/year family (verify 2026 IRS limits)

**QSEHRA vs ICHRA:**
- QSEHRA: Only for companies with no group plan; max allowance is IRS-capped; cannot vary allowances by employee class
- ICHRA: No group plan required; no IRS cap on allowance amount; allowances CAN vary by employee class (full-time, part-time, seasonal)
- **For most startups, ICHRA is more flexible than QSEHRA**

### Stage 2: Seed/early growth (5-25 employees)

**Best option:** Gusto Benefits or Rippling Benefits (if already on the platform).

- Both offer group health, dental, vision through major carriers (United, Aetna, BlueCross, Kaiser depending on region)
- Gusto Benefits: integrated with Gusto payroll; simple enrollment; competitive for small groups
- Rippling Benefits: integrated with Rippling; better for companies that are already on Rippling; stronger reporting
- **Key decision:** If you're on Gusto, use Gusto Benefits. If you're on Rippling, use Rippling Benefits. Don't mix platforms unless there's a compelling reason.

**What to offer at this stage:**
- Medical: required for competitive hiring; aim for 75-90% employer contribution on employee premiums (market standard for tech startups)
- Dental: add-on; relatively cheap ($20-40/employee/month); worth including
- Vision: add-on; cheap (~$5-10/employee/month); worth including
- 401(k): Gusto and Rippling both offer integrated 401(k) through partners; start this as soon as you have 5+ employees to avoid the talent disadvantage

### Stage 3: Series A / growth (25-100 employees)

**Options:**
- **Justworks PEO:** Access to ADP TotalSource-level group rates via the PEO umbrella. The PEO model means Justworks is the ACA employer of record; you get Fortune 500-level insurance rates because Justworks pools all its PEO clients together. Cost: ~$59-99/employee/month (includes payroll + benefits admin).
- **Brokered solution:** Hire a benefits broker (Sequoia Consulting, Mercer, NFP) to negotiate directly with carriers. Best for companies with 50+ employees who want carrier-level negotiating power without the PEO structure.
- **Rippling Benefits with self-insured option:** At 100+ employees, Rippling can administer self-insured health plans.

**When Justworks PEO beats Gusto/Rippling for benefits:**
Justworks's PEO model typically produces 10-20% lower premiums on health coverage for groups of 15-50 because of pooled negotiating power. Run the comparison: take your current or projected benefits cost and compare Justworks's all-in per-employee price vs Gusto/Rippling platform + broker cost.

### Stage 4: Late-stage / enterprise (100+ employees)

- Direct carrier contracts via a benefits broker
- Self-insured (stop-loss) plan becomes economically viable at 200+ employees
- Benefits administration via Rippling, Workday Benefits, or a standalone HRIS

## Remote team considerations

If the company has employees in multiple states:

- Group health plan networks are geography-dependent; a California-only Blue Shield network does not cover employees in New York
- **Best solutions for remote teams:**
  - ICHRA (each employee buys local coverage)
  - National carrier plans (United Healthcare, Aetna national network plans)
  - Gusto/Rippling national plan options (check carrier availability by state)
- At 10+ employees across 3+ states, getting broker help is worth the cost

## Retirement: 401(k) quick guidance

- Gusto, Rippling, and Justworks all offer integrated 401(k) setup through partners (Guideline, Human Interest, Betterment at Work)
- Safe Harbor 401(k): simplest plan design; eliminates most ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing; costs ~3% employer match
- Set up a 401(k) by Series A if competitive with the engineering talent market — the talent advantage is meaningful and the IRS limits for 2026 ($23,500 employee contribution, $70,000 total) make it a significant benefit
