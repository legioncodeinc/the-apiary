# 06 — Small-Team and Startup Adaptation

OKRs are powerful and add overhead. This guide determines the minimum viable OKR practice for teams under 20 people and identifies when to skip OKRs entirely.

*Sources: `research/external/03-radical-focus-wodtke-small-team.md`, `research/external/2026-05-20-okr-small-team-startup-adaptation.md`, `research/external/01-devokr-complete-okr-guide-2026.md`*

---

## The fit assessment: should this team use OKRs?

Before recommending OKRs to a small team, run this fit check:

| Factor | OKRs likely helpful | OKRs likely overhead |
|---|---|---|
| Team size | 5-50 people | 1-4 people |
| Clarity of mission | Directionally clear, multiple paths | Single obvious next thing |
| Planning horizon | Can plan 90 days out | Living week to week (pre-product-market fit) |
| Goal-setting history | Some history with goals | No goal-setting culture yet |
| Coordination need | Multiple workstreams, need alignment | Single workstream, everyone knows the plan |

**Recommendation:** If two or more "overhead" factors apply, suggest weekly priorities or a single company focus instead of OKRs. OKRs add value when teams need to align on WHAT matters before they can coordinate HOW.

Christina Wodtke's "Radical Focus" framing: "OKRs are a tool for teams that have clarity on their goal but not yet on their path to it."

## Minimum viable OKR practice (teams of 5-20)

### Tier 1: One company OKR (recommended starting point)

Start with exactly **one Objective and three Key Results** at the company level. No team-level OKRs. No individual OKRs. No cascade.

Why: Cascade adds coordination cost. Before a team has practiced two OKR cycles, cascade produces bureaucracy, not alignment.

**Format:**
```
Objective: [Aspirational, qualitative, cycle-scoped sentence]

KR1: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [cycle end]
KR2: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [cycle end]
KR3: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [cycle end]
```

**Cycle length:** Quarterly is the default. Monthly cycles are appropriate for teams in fast-moving early-stage phases (pre-PMF, early growth).

### Tier 2: One company OKR + optional team OKRs

Once the team has completed 2-3 successful OKR cycles and has a culture of honest scoring, individual team leads may optionally author team OKRs. Rules:
- Team OKRs must support at least one company KR explicitly (note the connection)
- Team OKRs are OPTIONAL — only create them when there is genuine alignment need
- No more than 2 Objectives per team
- No individual-level OKRs until the team is 30+ people

### Tier 3: Full cascade (not recommended below 30 people)

Company → department → team → individual. This is the Google model, appropriate for companies above ~100 people. Below 30 people, cascade produces overhead that outweighs alignment benefit.

## Cadence adaptations for small teams

| Practice | Standard cadence | Small-team adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| Check-ins | Weekly per team | Monthly for company OKR; skip team check-ins |
| Mid-quarter | Week 6-7 review | Mid-month review if on monthly cycles |
| Grading ceremony | 60-90 min formal session | 30 min informal; one person shares, rest react |
| Tools | Lattice, 15Five, Weekdone | Notion template or a Google Sheet is fine |
| CFR | Structured 1:1 cadence | Weekly team sync; 1:1 is sufficient for CFR |

## When to skip OKRs entirely

Recommend against OKRs (and name a better alternative) in these situations:

**1. Pre-product-market fit startups (0-3 people, single focus)**
- What to use instead: A single "north star metric" and weekly priorities.
- Why: OKRs optimize execution, not discovery. Pre-PMF teams need learning loops, not execution alignment.

**2. Crisis mode / survival quarter**
- What to use instead: A single 30-day priority list.
- Why: If the company is managing cash flow or a major incident, OKR cadence overhead is a distraction.

**3. Teams that have never done any goal-setting**
- What to do first: Run one quarter with informal goals and retrospective. Then introduce the OKR format.
- Why: OKRs fail when dropped into a culture with no goal-setting habits. Build the habit first.

**4. Teams that resist OKRs after 2 failed cycles**
- What to investigate: Are the OKRs being linked to compensation? Are scores being used in reviews? Is the format being over-engineered?
- Fix the root cause. If the root cause is cultural (leadership does not model OKRs), OKRs will not work until leadership changes.

## The Radical Focus pattern (Wodtke)

Christina Wodtke's adaptation (from "Radical Focus") for startup teams:

- One Objective per quarter. Not two, not three.
- Three Key Results that, if all hit, prove the Objective was achieved.
- A weekly "Monday OKR standup" (15 minutes): What did we commit to this week? What confidence (1-10) do we have in the Objective?
- A Friday "celebration and retrospective": What shipped? What learned?

This is the minimum functional OKR practice. It produces the cadence benefit (regular goal reflection) without the enterprise overhead.
