# Example: Happy Path — New B2B SaaS Blog

**Scenario:** A solo founder has just launched a B2B SaaS product for engineering managers (project management + sprint tooling). No existing blog content. Goal: build topical authority to drive organic demo signups. Team: the founder, 5 hours/week available.

This example walks end-to-end through one blogging session with `blogging-content-strategy-worker-bee`.

---

## Step 1: Business context confirmation

Before any content decisions:

- **Product:** Sprint planning tool for engineering managers
- **Audience:** Engineering managers at 20-200 person companies
- **Goal:** Organic traffic → demo signup
- **Team size:** Solo founder
- **Hours/week for content:** 5 (with AI writing assistance)
- **Domain authority:** Brand new domain (DA 0)
- **Keyword tool access:** Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (free, verified) + Google Search Console

---

## Step 2: Cluster hypothesis

Based on audience + product, the worker-bee proposes 2 cluster candidates for a new domain:

**Cluster A: "Sprint planning for engineering teams"**
- Pillar: "The engineering manager's guide to sprint planning"
- Reasoning: High intent alignment with product; manageable difficulty for new domain; audience pain point confirmed by product research

**Cluster B: "Engineering team productivity"**
- Pillar: "Engineering team productivity: the complete guide"
- Reasoning: Broader topical area but less direct conversion path

**Decision:** Start with Cluster A only (solo founder, 2-4 posts/month sustainable cadence). Cluster B is queued for month 4-6 after Cluster A has 8+ posts published.

## Step 6: AEO formatting applied to pillar H2 example

**Before (no AEO):**
> ### What is sprint planning?
> Sprint planning is a ceremony that happens at the start of every sprint. Teams use it to decide what they're going to work on. There's a lot of variation in how teams run it. Some teams do it in an hour, some take half a day...

**After (AEO-formatted):**
> ### What is sprint planning?
> Sprint planning is a Scrum ceremony where the engineering team selects and commits to a set of backlog items for the upcoming sprint, producing a sprint goal and a sprint backlog. Effective sprint planning meetings run 60-90 minutes for 2-week sprints and result in a committed sprint backlog with clear acceptance criteria.
>
> Sprint planning serves two purposes: (1) alignment on what gets built this sprint, and (2) a shared understanding of the "why" behind the sprint goal...

The second version leads with a 53-word definition block that is standalone, cites no external source (because it is factual), and gives Google/AI systems a complete answer to "what is sprint planning" before expanding into detail.

## Step 7: CTA

**Post:** "How to run a sprint planning meeting"
**Funnel stage:** Awareness (informational post)
**Primary action:** Demo signup

**Bad CTA:** "If you found this helpful, sign up for our newsletter to get more content like this!"
- Fails "why now" test
- Uses banned pattern "if you found this helpful"
- Implies the reader owes a favor

**Good CTA:** "Plan your next sprint in half the time. [Try [Product Name] free for 14 days — no credit card required.]"
- Names the outcome ("plan your next sprint in half the time")
- Zero beg language
- Removes the primary barrier ("no credit card required")
